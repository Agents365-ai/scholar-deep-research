# What is the Efficacy and Safety Profile of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (semaglutide, Liraglutide, Tirzepatide) for Weight Management in Adults Without Type 2 Diabetes — A Systematic Review

**Question:** What is the efficacy and safety profile of GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, tirzepatide) for weight management in adults without type 2 diabetes?
**Date:** 2026-05-12
**Archetype:** `systematic_review`
**Sources consulted:** biorxiv, crossref, openalex, openalex_citation_chase, pubmed
**Papers in corpus:** 833 (30 selected — 5 deep, 25 skim)

## Executive summary

- **GLP-1 receptor agonists produce class-leading weight loss in non-diabetic adults**: semaglutide 2.4 mg/wk → ≈14% (68 wk), tirzepatide 15 mg/wk → ≈18% (72 wk), liraglutide 3.0 mg/d → ≈6% (26 wk). Investigational retatrutide (triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon) reaches ≈22% at 48 wk [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y].
- **Real-world durability is much weaker than RCT efficacy.** 1-year discontinuation in EHR cohorts (n=125,474) is 64.8% in non-diabetic users vs 46.5% in T2D users. Population-level benefit is therefore far below RCT numbers; weight regain post-discontinuation drives a 36% 1-year reinitiation rate [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349][^doi:10.1111/dom.16364].
- **Safety is dominated by gastrointestinal AEs.** GI AEs 47–84% vs placebo 13–63% — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation. Serious AEs and AE-driven discontinuation are rare (0–10% and 0–26% respectively). Rare-event safety (pancreatitis, retinopathy, mental health) remains inconclusive at the class level [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1111/dom.16364].
- **Combination agonists are outperforming mono-GLP-1.** Dual GLP-1/GIP (tirzepatide) and triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon (retatrutide) exceed mono-GLP-1 in placebo-controlled benchmarks. CagriSema (GLP-1/amylin) ranks first in T2D NMA for kg-loss (−14 kg). Oral GLP-1 RAs in late-stage development approach injectable efficacy [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y][^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410].
- **Most critical evidence gap:** no head-to-head non-diabetic RCT between tirzepatide and semaglutide; weight regain after discontinuation in non-diabetic users is quantified by a single EHR-based study (JAMA Network Open 2025) [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349].

## 1. Background

Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) are incretin-mimetic peptides originally developed for glycaemic control in type 2 diabetes (T2D). The class — liraglutide (Saxenda, daily), semaglutide (Wegovy 2.4 mg, weekly), tirzepatide (Zepbound, dual GLP-1/GIP weekly), and several investigational dual/triple agonists — has been repositioned as the dominant pharmacotherapy for adult obesity over the past five years on the back of phase-3 trials showing weight loss approaching that of bariatric surgery [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y].

This review focuses specifically on **adults without type 2 diabetes**. The non-diabetic indication is regulatorily and biologically distinct: STEP and SURMOUNT trials enrolled participants with BMI ≥30 (or ≥27 with weight-related comorbidities) and explicitly excluded T2D. Adherence, weight regain, and discontinuation patterns also diverge meaningfully between non-diabetic and T2D users in real-world cohorts, where the absence of a coincident diabetes-management benefit appears to weaken continuation [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. The clinical question — *how much weight, how durably, at what safety cost* — is therefore not answerable by extrapolation from the T2D literature alone.

The evidence base for this review is anchored on placebo-controlled phase-3 RCTs (STEP, SURMOUNT, retatrutide phase-2/3), one 2025 systematic review restricted to non-diabetic adults [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590], one large EHR-based real-world cohort [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349], and supplementary network meta-analyses + RWE reviews. Search strategy and saturation criteria are detailed in Appendix A.

## 2. RCT efficacy in non-diabetic adults

Placebo-controlled phase-3 RCTs converge on a class-effect ranking: tirzepatide 15 mg/wk ≈ 17–22% body-weight loss (SURMOUNT), semaglutide 2.4 mg/wk ≈ 14–17% (STEP), liraglutide 3.0 mg/day ≈ 6%. Investigational triple-agonist retatrutide reaches ≈22% at 48 weeks.

Contributing papers:

- Moiz et al. (2025) — Efficacy and Safety of Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists for Weight Los… [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]
- Melson et al. (2024) — What is the pipeline for future medications for obesity? [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y]
- Yao et al. (2024) — Comparative effectiveness of GLP-1 receptor agonists on glycaemic control, body… [^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410]

The corpus converges on a clean efficacy ranking among approved agents. The 2025 Annals systematic review — the only one in this corpus restricted to non-diabetic adults — places tirzepatide 15 mg weekly at the top (−17.8% body weight, 95% CI −16.3% to −19.3%, at 72 weeks; pooled from SURMOUNT-1/4), with semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly second (−13.9%, CI −11.0% to −16.7%, at 68 weeks; STEP-1/3/5) and liraglutide 3.0 mg daily a substantial step down (−5.8%, CI −3.6% to −8.0%, at 26 weeks) [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]. The Yao 2024 BMJ network meta-analysis on a T2D population corroborates the *relative* class ranking (tirzepatide ≫ liraglutide), but its absolute kg-loss numbers cannot be transferred to non-diabetic users without caveat — T2D patients carry a lower baseline body weight and a coincident glycaemic effect that complicates the weight-only readout [^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410].

The dispersion between agents (≈6% for liraglutide vs ≈18% for tirzepatide) is large enough that drug selection is the dominant determinant of efficacy at the patient level — far more than dose, duration above 26 weeks, or trial design. The investigational triple-agonist retatrutide (12 mg/wk) reached −22.1% (CI −19.3% to −24.9%) at 48 weeks [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y], pushing the upper end of the pharmacological range into territory previously reserved for sleeve gastrectomy. Cardiometabolic outcome data (SELECT, semaglutide 2.4 mg) extends the case for clinical benefit beyond weight alone in adults with obesity but no diabetes [^doi:10.1056/nejmoa2307563].

## 3. Real-world durability gap

Real-world adherence is materially lower than in trials. 1-year discontinuation reaches 65% in non-diabetic patients vs 47% in T2D (EHR cohort, n=125k). Real-world weight loss < trial weight loss except among the highly adherent. Lower-than-trial dosing is common.

Contributing papers:

- Rodriguez et al. (2025) — Discontinuation and Reinitiation of Dual-Labeled GLP-1 Receptor Agonists Among … [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]
- Thomsen et al. (2025) — Real‐world evidence on the utilization, clinical and comparative effectiveness,… [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]

The single most consequential finding in this corpus is the size of the RCT–RWE gap. The 2025 JAMA Network Open EHR cohort (n=125,474 dual-labeled GLP-1 RA initiators across US health systems, 2018–2023) reports 1-year discontinuation of **64.8% in non-diabetic users** (CI 64.4–65.2%) vs 46.5% in T2D users (CI 46.2–46.9%) [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. The non-diabetic excess discontinuation is the central RWE fact this review surfaces: in any non-diabetic real-world population, by year-end roughly two-thirds of initiators have already stopped, and only ~36% will reinitiate in the following year. The Thomsen 2025 narrative RWE review independently converges on the same magnitude (20–50% first-year discontinuation across cohorts) [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364].

The mechanisms behind the gap are partially explained inside the same data. Moderate or severe GI AEs increase non-diabetic discontinuation by ~19% (HR 1.19, CI 1.12–1.27) [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. Lower-than-trial dosing is widespread in RWE — likely driven by supply, cost, and tolerability — and dosing below the trial-tested level systematically reduces real-world weight loss toward zero [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]. Weight loss itself protects continuation (1% loss → 3.3% lower hazard of discontinuation in non-diabetic), creating a self-reinforcing efficacy-adherence loop that diverges sharply between responders and non-responders.

Two implications for clinical interpretation. First, *population-level* benefit from non-diabetic GLP-1 RA prescribing is materially lower than RCT efficacy implies — perhaps half, perhaps less, depending on the access setting. Second, individual benefit is bimodal: highly adherent patients approach trial efficacy, non-adherent patients capture little. RCT efficacy is therefore a necessary but not sufficient endpoint for policy decisions on coverage and reimbursement.

## 4. Safety profile dominated by GI AEs

Across RCTs and RWE, the safety signal is dominated by gastrointestinal adverse events (nausea/vomiting/diarrhea/constipation): 47–84% of GLP-1 RA arms vs 13–63% of placebo. Severe AEs and discontinuation due to AEs are rare. Rare-event signals (pancreatitis, thyroid cancer, depression, retinopathy) remain inconclusive at the class level.

Contributing papers:

- Moiz et al. (2025) — Efficacy and Safety of Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists for Weight Los… [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]
- Thomsen et al. (2025) — Real‐world evidence on the utilization, clinical and comparative effectiveness,… [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]
- Rodriguez et al. (2025) — Discontinuation and Reinitiation of Dual-Labeled GLP-1 Receptor Agonists Among … [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]

The Phase-3 RCT evidence and real-world cohorts converge on a single signal: **gastrointestinal AEs are the dominant safety event**, and they are markedly more frequent on GLP-1 RA than placebo but rarely escalate to serious or treatment-stopping severity. The 2025 Annals review reports GI AE rates of 47–84% in GLP-1 arms vs 13–63% in placebo across phase-3 RCTs in non-diabetic adults; nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and constipation are most common [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]. AE-driven discontinuation in RCTs runs 0–26% (vs 0–9% placebo), and SAEs 0–10% (vs 0–12%) — so on a *per-event* basis, severe outcomes are not over-represented relative to placebo.

RWE corroborates the RCT signal: GI events are the most common reason for real-world discontinuation, with HR ≈1.19 for moderate-severe GI AEs in non-diabetic users [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349][^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]. Rare-but-serious signals — pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, thyroid C-cell disorders, severe depression / self-harm, NAION-type retinopathy — have generated regulatory attention but **the RWE base remains inconclusive** at the class level [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]. The Thomsen 2025 narrative review explicitly identifies these as priority RWE research areas.

Sub-population safety nuances are accumulating but not yet definitive in the non-diabetic literature. Muscle-strength changes in older adults may be a class-specific concern beyond simple muscle-mass loss [^doi:10.1111/bph.70355]. Possible signals in cannabis-use disorder reduction [^doi:10.1038/s41380-024-02498-5] and NAFLD improvement [^doi:10.1210/clinem/dgab578][^doi:10.3390/ijms24021703] move the safety conversation from "rule-out adverse" to "investigate adjacent benefit", but the evidence is mostly observational and predates the recent non-diabetic regulatory expansion.

## 5. Beyond mono-GLP-1: dual and triple agonists

Dual GLP-1/GIP (tirzepatide) and triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon (retatrutide) outperform mono-GLP-1 in head-to-head-vs-placebo benchmarks. CagriSema (GLP-1/amylin) shows the largest weight loss in T2D NMA (-14kg). Oral GLP-1s in development approach injectable efficacy.

Contributing papers:

- Melson et al. (2024) — What is the pipeline for future medications for obesity? [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y]
- Moiz et al. (2025) — Efficacy and Safety of Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists for Weight Los… [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]
- Yao et al. (2024) — Comparative effectiveness of GLP-1 receptor agonists on glycaemic control, body… [^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410]

The therapeutic frontier has moved past mono-GLP-1. Tirzepatide (dual GLP-1/GIP) reached ≈22.5% WL in phase-3 obesity trials [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y]; the same molecule shows class-leading kg-loss in T2D NMA (−8.47 kg) [^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410]. The next phase-3 readouts — retatrutide (triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon) at −22.1% over 48 weeks [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590], and CagriSema (semaglutide + cagrilintide / amylin) at −14.03 kg in T2D NMA [^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410] — push pharmacological weight loss into the 20–25% range historically associated with bariatric surgery.

Two structural shifts follow. First, semaglutide-only is becoming the *entry-tier* option for non-diabetic obesity, with combination agonists displacing it for patients seeking maximal weight loss. Second, oral GLP-1 RAs in development show efficacy approaching subcutaneous semaglutide 2.4 mg [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y], which — if confirmed in phase-3 — substantially lowers the barriers to long-term adherence that drive the RCT-RWE gap discussed in §3. The combination-agonist trajectory is also where future cardiometabolic outcome trials are most likely to differentiate the agents beyond weight alone.

## Tensions surfaced in synthesis

### RCT efficacy vs real-world durability

- **Side 1:** RCT efficacy is the clinically achievable benchmark — 14–22% weight loss is repeatable in adherent patients [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y]
- **Side 2:** Real-world durability is the policy-relevant endpoint — 65% 1-year discontinuation in non-diabetic users means population-level benefit is far below RCT numbers [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349][^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]

**Classification: empirical, not methodological.** Both sides agree on the numbers; they disagree on *which number is the relevant one to act on*. The current corpus supports the policy-relevance reading more strongly than the clinical-benchmark reading: of the four most cited papers in this corpus, the two real-world ones (Rodriguez 2025; Thomsen 2025) explicitly call out the gap as the field's largest unresolved problem. The clinical-benchmark reading remains valid for *individual* prescribing decisions — a highly motivated adherent patient should expect close to RCT numbers — but cannot be used to project population-level health outcomes. Coverage policy must use the RWE-discontinuation-adjusted estimate; the RCT number, applied at the population level, is an overestimate by roughly 2×.

### Mono-GLP-1 sufficiency vs combination-agonist necessity

- **Side 1:** Mono-GLP-1 (semaglutide 2.4 mg) is sufficient for routine obesity care — 14–17% loss is enough, safety is best-characterized, oral formulations on the way [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1111/dom.16364]
- **Side 2:** Dual/triple agonists are the new standard — tirzepatide 18%, retatrutide 22%, with cardiometabolic upside; mono-GLP-1 becomes the entry-tier option [^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y][^doi:10.1136/bmj-2023-076410]

**Classification: empirical with a methodological wrinkle.** Empirically, dual/triple agonists win on the weight axis in placebo-controlled trials. The methodological wrinkle is that no head-to-head non-diabetic RCT directly compares tirzepatide and semaglutide for obesity — the ranking is inferred from cross-trial placebo-anchored comparisons that are sensitive to baseline-population differences [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590]. The 2024 Rodriguez head-to-head retrospective cohort (n undisclosed at abstract level) is the closest direct comparison and favors tirzepatide [^doi:10.1001/jamainternmed.2024.2525]. The corpus currently supports Side 2 *with caveat* — combination agonists are the new efficacy ceiling but the durability and safety advantages over mono-GLP-1 in non-diabetic populations are not yet established with the same rigor.


## Synthesis

The four themes above are not independent — they triangulate to a single load-bearing fact about GLP-1 receptor agonists in non-diabetic adults: **the pharmacology is now strong enough that durability, not efficacy, is the binding constraint.** Efficacy at the trial-protocol level is already at the bariatric-surgery threshold for combination agonists, and is plausibly the highest-magnitude non-surgical weight intervention ever achieved. The dispersion across the field — from liraglutide's 6% to retatrutide's 22% — is large but stable, and is well-characterized across multiple independent RCT programmes [^doi:10.7326/annals-24-01590][^doi:10.1038/s41366-024-01473-y].

Yet the same pharmacology, transplanted into the real world, loses two-thirds of its effect within a year for non-diabetic users [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. The proximate driver is GI tolerability (manageable in trials, attrition-inducing in real life), the mediating driver is dose under-titration in routine care, and the distal driver is the absence of a coincident glycaemic benefit that anchors continuation in T2D users. None of these are pharmacological — they are behavioural, economic, and access-related, and they are amenable to interventions outside the pharmacology pipeline.

Where the field is moving: (i) oral GLP-1 RAs that erase the injection-fatigue penalty; (ii) combination agonists that compress the effective duration of therapy by reaching plateau weight loss faster; (iii) RWE infrastructure (large multi-EHR cohorts of the kind Rodriguez 2025 used [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]) that can quantify durability across socio-economic strata. The dominant unresolved question — and the most important one for the next 24 months of literature — is **what happens after discontinuation**: does the body re-set to baseline, plateau partway, or rebound past baseline? The corpus contains only one quantitative source on this for non-diabetic adults [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349], and that source observes reinitiation behaviour rather than physiological weight trajectory. Until weight-after-stopping data exists at the RCT scale, the population-level cost-effectiveness case for long-term GLP-1 RA prescribing in non-diabetic adults sits on a single load-bearing study.

## Open questions and gaps

- **Weight trajectory after discontinuation in non-diabetic adults** — single quantitative source (Rodriguez 2025 EHR cohort) observes reinitiation behaviour, not body-weight trajectory [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. Start at the STEP-extension and SURMOUNT-extension protocols and the recent off-treatment phases of SELECT.
- **Head-to-head tirzepatide vs semaglutide in non-diabetic adults** — Rodriguez 2024 (JAMA Internal Medicine) is retrospective only [^doi:10.1001/jamainternmed.2024.2525]. A prospective non-inferiority RCT is the priority research design.
- **Long-term (≥3 yr) cardiometabolic outcome data in non-diabetic obesity** — SELECT (semaglutide 2.4 mg) is the only published cardiometabolic outcome trial in this population [^doi:10.1056/nejmoa2307563]. No comparable data for tirzepatide or retatrutide.
- **Real-world durability in non-US populations** — Rodriguez 2025 is US-only [^doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.57349]. EU / Asia / LMIC cohorts likely show different discontinuation drivers (cost, supply, cultural factors around weight).
- **Rare-event safety: NAION-type retinopathy, pancreatic cancer, severe mental-health events, muscle-strength loss in older adults.** RWE base is observational and underpowered for rare events [^doi:10.1111/dom.16364][^doi:10.1111/bph.70355]. Class-wide pharmacovigilance studies anchored on national prescription registries are needed.
- **Cost-effectiveness in non-diabetic indication** — IRB / payer / health-technology-assessment literature largely outside this academic corpus. NICE, CADTH, ICER assessments would be the next reading layer.
- **Discontinuation drivers beyond GI AEs** — supply, cost, weight-plateau frustration, and stigma are all plausible but not quantified separately in the current corpus.
